Biography

Hedy Lamarr (November 9, 1914 – January 19, 2000) was an Austro-Hungarian-born American actress and technology inventor. She was a film star during Hollywood's Golden Age.

She and George Antheil invented a classified communication system especially useful for submarines.

The system was based on radio frequencies, changed at irregular periods, that were synchronized between the transmitter and receiver.

Early Life and Education:

Hedy Lamarr, born Hedwig Eva Maria Kiesler, was an Austrian-American actress and inventor. She was born in Vienna, Austria, on November 9, 1914. Lamarr began her acting career in European films before gaining international recognition.

Career:

Hedy Lamarr achieved fame as a Hollywood actress in the 1930s and 1940s, starring in notable films such as "Algiers" (1938) and "Boom Town" (1940). Despite her success in the film industry, Lamarr had a passion for science and technology.

During World War II, Lamarr, along with composer George Antheil, developed a frequency-hopping system intended for radio-controlled torpedoes. Their invention aimed to prevent the interception of torpedo guidance signals by enemies. Although their invention was not implemented during the war, the frequency-hopping technology laid the foundation for later developments in secure communications.

Achievements and Recognition:

Hedy Lamarr's contributions to technology were not widely recognized during her lifetime. However, in the later years of her life, she received acknowledgment for her role in the development of frequency-hopping technology. In 1997, she and George Antheil were honored with the Electronic Frontier Foundation Pioneer Award for their pioneering work.

Lamarr's impact extended beyond her acting career, and her inventive spirit left a lasting legacy in the field of secure communication technologies.

Later Years and Legacy:

Hedy Lamarr passed away on January 19, 2000, in Casselberry, Florida. Her innovative contributions to technology became more widely appreciated posthumously. In recognition of her achievements, Lamarr was posthumously inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame in 2014.

Hedy Lamarr's legacy encompasses both her significant contributions to the entertainment industry as an actress and her groundbreaking work in the field of secure communications. She is remembered as a multifaceted individual who made lasting impacts in both the arts and sciences.
