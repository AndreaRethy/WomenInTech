Biography

Early Life and Education:

Judith Milhon, best known by her pseudonym St. Jude, was born on March 12, 1939, in Washington, D.C., USA. She grew up to become a significant figure in the world of computing, known for her self-taught programming skills and advocacy for civil rights.

Career:

Milhon emerged as a self-taught programmer and hacker in the vibrant tech culture of the San Francisco Bay Area. Her expertise extended to various aspects of computing, and she became known for her contributions to the early hacker community. Milhon actively engaged in writing, editing, and advocating for women in computing during a time when the tech industry was predominantly male-dominated.

As a writer, she co-authored several books and articles that helped disseminate knowledge about computer programming and hacking. Her work played a role in fostering a sense of community and knowledge-sharing within the emerging hacker culture.

Achievements and Recognition:

Beyond her technical contributions, Judith Milhon was an advocate for civil rights and women's rights in the computing field. At a time when the industry lacked diversity, Milhon worked to break down barriers for women in technology. Her efforts contributed to creating a more inclusive space for individuals interested in computing, regardless of gender.

Milhon's advocacy was particularly influential in encouraging women to pursue careers in technology, leaving a lasting impact on the trajectory of the industry.

Later Years and Legacy:

Judith Milhon passed away on July 19, 2003. While her life was cut short, her legacy endures as a pioneering figure in the early days of computing and hacking. Milhon's contributions to writing, programming, and advocacy for women in technology continue to inspire those who follow in her footsteps.

Her work is remembered not only for its technical significance but also for the strides she made in promoting diversity and equality in the evolving world of computing. Judith Milhon's legacy remains an important chapter in the history of hacking and women's contributions to the technology industry.
